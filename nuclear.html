What Do Nuclear Engineers Do?

Nuclear engineers play a critical role in harnessing the power of atomic energy for peaceful and practical applications, including energy production, medical treatments, industrial applications, and national security. From designing nuclear reactors to improving radiation safety and advancing fusion energy research, nuclear engineers are at the forefront of innovation in one of the most powerful scientific fields. Applying principles of physics, thermodynamics, and materials science, nuclear engineers develop systems that safely control nuclear reactions, monitor radiation levels, and optimize reactor efficiency. They also work on fuel cycle technologies, nuclear waste management, and advanced medical imaging or cancer treatment systems like radiation therapy. In addition to detailed system design and technical calculations, nuclear engineers often use specialized simulation tools to model reactor behavior, radiation shielding, and emergency scenarios. These simulations help identify potential risks, optimize safety systems, and ensure compliance with rigorous international regulations. Nuclear engineers collaborate closely with professionals in mechanical, electrical, and chemical engineering as well as health physicists and government regulators to develop and maintain safe, effective nuclear systems for civilian and industrial use.

Job Titles in Nuclear Engineering

Job Title Role Description Typical Salary
Nuclear Reactor Engineer Designs, operates, and maintains nuclear reactor systems for energy production or research. Ensures reactor safety and efficiency. $75,000 – $115,000 per year
Radiation Protection Specialist Monitors and controls radiation exposure in medical, industrial, or energy settings to protect people and the environment. $65,000 – $95,000 per year
Nuclear Safety Analyst Conducts risk assessments and safety analysis for nuclear power plants and facilities. Prepares emergency procedures and compliance reports. $80,000 – $120,000 per year
Medical Physicist Applies nuclear technology in medicine, such as radiation therapy or diagnostic imaging. Works closely with healthcare professionals. $85,000 – $130,000 per year
Fuel Cycle Engineer Focuses on nuclear fuel management, from enrichment and fabrication to disposal and recycling of spent fuel. $70,000 – $110,000 per year
Fusion Research Scientist Works on developing future energy systems based on nuclear fusion. Involves plasma physics, high-energy systems, and experimental devices. $90,000 – $140,000 per year

These salary ranges are approximate and may vary depending on education level, experience, region, and sector (e.g., government, private industry, or academia).

Industries Where Nuclear Engineers Work

  • Energy Sector: Designing and operating nuclear power plants for electricity generation, including both fission and experimental fusion reactors.
  • Medical Sector: Developing and maintaining radiological equipment used in cancer treatment (e.g., radiotherapy) and diagnostic imaging (e.g., PET scans).
  • Defense and Aerospace: Working on nuclear propulsion for submarines or spacecraft, as well as national defense applications.
  • Nuclear Waste Management: Handling and disposing of radioactive waste materials safely and according to environmental standards.
  • Government and Regulation: Regulatory bodies such as the Nuclear Regulatory Commission (NRC) oversee nuclear safety and compliance with laws.
  • Research and Academia: Advancing nuclear science through experimental research and training the next generation of engineers and scientists.
  • Industrial and Manufacturing: Using radiation for non-destructive testing, sterilization of equipment, and materials engineering.

Skills You'll Need

Nuclear engineers need a well-rounded set of technical and analytical skills to manage the complexities and safety demands of nuclear systems:

  • Knowledge of Nuclear Physics and Thermodynamics: Understanding how nuclear reactions work and how energy is transferred in systems.
  • Radiation Protection and Safety Awareness: Skills in radiation shielding, dosimetry, and emergency response procedures.
  • Simulation and Modeling Tools: Use of specialized software for reactor design, neutron transport, and thermal-hydraulic analysis (e.g., MCNP, SCALE, RELAP5).
  • Critical Thinking and Risk Assessment: Analyzing safety risks and making sound decisions under uncertainty.
  • Communication and Teamwork: Collaborating across disciplines and communicating technical information clearly and accurately.
  • Ethics and Regulatory Knowledge: Understanding the legal, environmental, and societal responsibilities of working in nuclear fields.

    Free courses by the International Atomic Energy Agency on radiation safety, nuclear basics, and peaceful applications of nuclear science.

Beginner Projects You Can Try

Build a Cloud Chamber

Create a simple particle detector that makes radiation visible by showing the paths of alpha and beta particles. This introduces nuclear decay and ionizing radiation in a hands-on way.

Radiation Shielding Experiment

Test how different materials (lead, aluminum, water) block ionizing radiation using a Geiger counter and safe sources like bananas or lantern mantles.

Thermal Simulation of a Reactor Core

Use simulation software or simple heat transfer models to explore how reactor cores are cooled and how temperature affects reactor performance and safety.
