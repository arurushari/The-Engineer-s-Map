What Do Aerospace Engineers Do?

Aerospace engineers specialize in the design, development, testing, and production of aircraft, spacecraft, satellites, missiles, and related systems. They apply core engineering principles—such as aerodynamics, propulsion, structural mechanics, flight dynamics, thermodynamics, and materials science—to ensure performance, safety, and efficiency. Their responsibilities range from simulating airflow over wings using computational fluid dynamics (CFD) to analyzing structural integrity under extreme conditions. They also work on propulsion systems, improving jet engines and rocket motors to enhance speed, fuel efficiency, and environmental performance. Aerospace engineers frequently use advanced CAD and simulation software to create digital prototypes, which are tested virtually before physical models are built. They also play a key role in system integration—ensuring avionics, control systems, guidance, and communications work seamlessly. From designing Mars rovers and reusable rockets to improving unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) and commercial airliners, aerospace engineers drive innovation in both atmospheric and spaceflight technologies.

Job Titles in Aerospace Engineering

Job Title Role Description Typical Salary
Aerospace Design Engineer Designs aircraft and spacecraft components such as fuselage, wings, engines, and control surfaces. Works with CAD and simulation tools to model performance. $75,000 – $110,000 per year
Flight Test Engineer Plans and conducts tests on aircraft or spacecraft to verify performance and safety. Analyzes data from sensors during test flights and suggests design improvements. $80,000 – $115,000 per year
Propulsion Engineer Specializes in developing jet engines, rocket propulsion systems, and related technologies. Focuses on efficiency, reliability, and thermal management. $85,000 – $120,000 per year
Avionics Engineer Designs electronic systems used for navigation, communication, and control in aircraft and spacecraft. Works closely with software and systems engineers. $75,000 – $105,000 per year
Space Systems Engineer Develops systems for satellites, space probes, and orbital platforms. Coordinates mechanical, electrical, and thermal subsystems to function in harsh environments. $90,000 – $130,000 per year

Salaries vary based on education, experience, location, and company size. Aerospace engineering is a high-demand field with significant opportunities in both private and government sectors.

Industries Where Aerospace Engineers Work

  • Commercial Aviation: Designing and improving airplanes for passenger and cargo transport, focusing on efficiency, safety, and performance.
  • Defense and Military: Developing advanced aircraft, drones, and missile systems for national security and strategic defense operations.
  • Space Exploration: Working with agencies like NASA or private companies like SpaceX to build rockets, satellites, space probes, and planetary landers.
  • Research and Development: Pushing the limits of aerospace technology in labs and research centers through testing and innovation.
  • Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs): Designing drones for commercial, agricultural, medical, and military use.
  • Satellite Communications: Building and maintaining satellites that provide GPS, weather forecasting, and global communications.
  • Manufacturing: Collaborating with suppliers and factories to build aerospace components and ensure quality control standards are met.

Skills You'll Need

  • Strong Background in Physics and Math: Especially in fluid dynamics, thermodynamics, and control systems.
  • Proficiency in CAD and Simulation Tools: Such as CATIA, ANSYS, MATLAB, or Simulink.
  • Analytical and Problem-Solving Skills: To evaluate flight performance and troubleshoot system errors.
  • Team Collaboration: Projects often involve multidisciplinary teams working together.
  • Attention to Detail: Precision is critical for safety and performance in aerospace applications.
  • Understanding of Materials Science: Especially high-performance composites and lightweight alloys.
  • Knowledge of Regulations: Familiarity with FAA, NASA, or ESA standards for compliance and safety.

Beginner Projects You Can Try

Water Rocket with Parachute and Data Logging

EBuild a water-powered rocket with a parachute that deploys at peak height. Add an Arduino altimeter to track flight data and learn about thrust, stability, and recovery systems. affect flight distance and stability. A fun way to explore basic flight mechanics.

Balsa Wood Glider

Design and build a lightweight glider to explore lift, drag, and balance. Test how different wing shapes and weight placements affect flight distance and stability.

Satellite Orbit Simulation using Python

Create a simple Python program to simulate a satellite orbiting Earth. Learn basic orbital mechanics and visualize how gravity and velocity shape an object’s path in space.
